Aims

To utilize the least amount of time within a medical appointment without sacrificing quality by using smart devices to our advantage. Achieving our aim will make doctor’s visits convenient, easy and fast, by giving the doctor/patient more time available to focus on the consult instead of wasting important time on other details.

For us to reach our aim we need to break down a doctor’s appointment in a few steps. From ringing to make an appointment, dislocating to the clinic’s location, waiting to see the doctor, providing physical data like blood pressure, ECG, weight, body mass and gathering history data and information about a patient. Each of these steps consumes time, so we will set our goals to shorten or extinguish the average time to complete these tasks by integrating digital solutions through our mobile phone app.

Goals

  • To eliminate travel and waiting time: by making video and audio appointments available in our app. With this function the patient won’t need to leave their house to have a consultation, saving travel and waiting time also making a doctor’s appointment convenient and easy. The fact that this patient is at home and there’s one less car on the road also helps our planet, reducing our carbon footprint.
  • To be able to send digital prescriptions and referrals: We can add to our app standard prescriptions and referrals that can be sent digitally to the patient or straight to the pharmacy where the patient can then collect. Some people need to make regular appointments just to get prescription medication, for these people having our request referral/prescription function in our app will save them a lot of time.
  • Using smart devices to do regular physical check-ups: This is a glimpse into the future where IoT devices will be interconnected talking to each other. In our health app, we plan to get any smart devices that collect personal data like digital watches, digital scales and use them towards inputting patients’ data into our appointment. By doing this our health professionals will not need to spend time doing physical check-ups.
  • Having digital patient’s history data available for health professionals: Having all the data, the professional needs right in front of them can help the appointment quality, saving the doctor or front desk from having to call or email other clinics to chase the patient’s medical history.

To identify the most important parts of the project, that will have priority over the others we need to look closely at our aim, which is to consume less time. Considering the biggest chunk of time from a patient’s point of view is wasted in dislocation (in some cases it takes longer to travel to the clinic’s location than the appointment itself) our priority will be setting up video/audio appointments and digital prescriptions/referrals.

11. Setup firewalls, router, and switches

The network equipment will be setup and configured by the network engineer with input from the security advisor. The network configuration will consist of 4 four CheckPoint firewalls configured in a failover cluster. CheckPoint firewalls were chosen for their ease of configuration and scalability (up to 52 firewalls) [5]. Beneath the firewalls in the stack will be 2 Cisco network routers in a failover configuration [2]. The routers will connect to the internal network switches which will be Cisco Catalyst 3750 stack switches [6]. The switches will be directly connected to the servers running OpenStack.

12. Build servers and load OS

The physical servers being used in the project are Dell PowerEdge R830 rack servers which were chosen because they are designed for virtualisation, with 4 CPU sockets, and 48 DIMM slots for up to 64GB DDR4 RAM in each slot [2]. The servers will be built and have the OS loaded onto them by the OpenStack specialist to ensure the base configuration meets the best practices for OpenStack.
The virtual servers will be built and configured by the Linux Engineer. At the initial implementation stage of the project, there will be 6 virtual machines, 2 each for databases, front-end web, and the backend API’s. Each pair of servers will be configured in high availability failover clusters. This approach allows for easy scaling as the virtual machines can be cloned and running in production within minutes.

13. Setup storage

The storage appliances chosen for this project are TrueNAS storage appliances which will be configured in a high availability cluster. The underlying disks of the storage arrays will be configured in RAID 6 disk arrays. The ZFS file system will be used to format the storage pools on the disks. ZFS is a widely used filesystem in the *nix world and has snapshot and replication capabilities. In addition to the afore mentioned, TrueNAS allows the running of Linux containers which can be used to run services such as Apache2 [2].

14. Setup OpenStack

The setup of OpenStack will be performed by the OpenStack specialist. Five servers will be used for OpenStack, 2 for the management of the OpenStack environment and 3 for backend services. This can be expanded in the future should the need arise.

15. Configure web servers

The web servers will also be configured by the Linux Engineer with input from the Security advisor. The web servers will be Apache2 running on Ubuntu OS.

16. Configure databases

The DBA will use the data model to configure the databases. The Security advisor will assist as this is the critical data of patients that cannot be exploited under any circumstances. A breach of client information would most likely end the App due to a suffering reputation and possible legal implications including being taken to court for damages.

They ensure that tasks are done at the correct time, on budget and the scope is adhered to. Any changes to the scope or workload are identified in time and discussed with the Project Sponsor. Scope and Limits

Back to Top

Scope is the division of work to be performed inside the project, no matter if the job is being done internally or outsourced. It must be a Scope, so the team and professionals are working on the same page.
One of the biggest issues when working on a team is often caused by discrepancies between peers, and to avoid that a scope is created. As part of the scoping, the project team will list out what is to be excluded and not part of the build. This provides a clear boundary of what the project scope entails.
The project team will list down the assumptions made. These provide a sounding board if any issues arise. In this case our scope consists in:

What needs to be done?

It appears to be an easy answer, but when dealing with a big project and a large team with lots of different areas, the project must be divided per skills and professionals that are able to develop and make the App run.

How should it be done?

Yes, this is very important to get the project running smooth, if one member of the team uses a different software than the rest of the team, it could be a big problem down the track once everything is put together. A plan and instructions on how the job need to be done is crucial for the project success.

How long should it take?

A time frame must be set. Anything can happen when dealing with technology, people can get sick and even leave the job on the middle of the project, for that reason a time frame and deadlines should be set on the start of the project to make sure that problems that comes up through it can be sorted on time.
Limits are very hard to set, it can depend on the risks, budget, professional skills, and market acceptance. Scope also tends to define limits to what lengths we should go to.
All projects have risks, but inside the scope a limit for those risks should be set (our risk appetite), for example if there is a risk of fraud or data leak inside the App, a budget for Cyber Security is set and if the team thinks that it is too expensive for the project there is a limit.
Or if there is a lack of professionals available on the market and the existing staff is not enough to finish by a deadline, a limit is placed.
One of the limits when building the projects would be a device target, for example if the data analysis shows that this type of App is mostly used in Mobile phones, we would set a limit that users can only download from Apps on a mobile device, excluding errors that could occur when downloading the App in a Tablet for example.
Limitations on budget would also be welcomed once the prototype is ready and tested, so we would know how we would make money on the App and if the figures could give us a profit.

When it comes to developing an App, testing the project is one of the most important steps for success. It is the part of the scope where you can see the weakness of the project and fix the issues you have before investing time and money on it.

There are many types of tests that can be performed on Mobile Applications. On the technical and developing aspects, usability, compatibility, interface, services, performance and security testing are the main ones when developing an App the will be launched and used by a large portion of the community, these tests would ensure that the App is easy to use and provides a good user experience, if it works on different devices, screens and operational systems, if all the menu and buttons are working accordingly, if it will work in different connections like 3G, 4G, 5G and Wi-Fi, if it uses the device battery in excess and if it needs some sort of log-in, validation and the system protects the data or not.

Once the technical part is running smooth, it is now time to check if real users are happy with it. You can start small and tell some friends and family to download your App and try to use it and give you feedback, or even check how similar Apps are being accept on the OS storer, that will also give you an idea of the issues and ideas that users are having when looking at the feedbacks.

As our App involves medical practices and Doctors as well as the patients, three tests would be essential for the App. If a doctor cannot use it correctly the patient would have misleading information as well as the opposite. Thinking about it testing in a small Medical Centre would be the best for this purpose, the app would be available for the staff and the patients of that practice and from there the feedbacks would be constructive so it can escalate for more people and places.

If the budget agrees that are some companies that offer a testing program doing paid survey.

Group Processes and Communications

Back to Top

The Magpies group chose Microsoft Teams as our repository for storing documents, scheduling meetings, and communicating among group members. We were able to transition between different communication modalities on this platform, such as video conferencing, instant messaging, document collaboration, and file sharing. It also functioned as a reminder calendar. Meetings were organised by Teams, and each meeting had a designated individual who took minutes. The meetings were recorded so that we could go back, watch, and listen to what we discussed. Meeting minutes were posted to the Teams folders. Teams was used as a collaboration tool to discuss concerns and receive peer reviews of the assigned task, throughout A2, A3, and A5. The group met twice a week using Teams meetings. Chats and individual conversations are help between group members to discuss their work and obtain feedback. On the final week of the assignment, we decided to have daily catch-up meetings to ensure we are also finalising the work to be submitted. Teams is available on phones and PC’s and hence formed the best tool to use. It also helps when you are on the move as your mobile device is always with you.

Our meetings were recorded in Teams so members who were not available could watch the recording and identify what transpired and work out what needed to be done prior to the next meeting.

The Magpies also used GitHub as a collaboration platform. When one of our group members wanted to incorporate one of his or her tasks into the group leader's repository or on a Word document, we used this as a Pull Request. Furthermore, if a team member is unable to continue working due to a challenging issue, he or she can send a pull request for assistance to the rest of the team.

Good communication includes going above and beyond to achieve progress. Checking in with your group members requires more than just making sure they're doing their best. When it come to an assigned task, your group members may appear productive, but they may later announce that they have given up. When this happens, the gap takes longer to close. Hence if you check in with the group members on a regular basis and address concerns, you can resolve them before they become major issues.

When we move to the build phase of the project, we will use the same methodology. Initial stages there will be frequent meetings held to bed the process and identify requirements and document processes. We have built a Communications and Engagement plan which will be used to provide communications within the development team and externally. Our commercial and marketing resource will ensure the project is conveyed to potential clients so that to gain an insight into where our focus would be in future.
